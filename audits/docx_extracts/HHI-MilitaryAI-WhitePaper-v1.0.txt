Abstract

Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming military and national
security capabilities — from intelligence analysis and logistics to
autonomous systems and cyber operations. This white paper examines the
dual imperative facing governments: maintaining strategic advantage
while preventing misuse, escalation, and systemic harm. Drawing on a
governance-first framework, the paper surveys current and emerging
military AI use cases across intelligence, surveillance and
reconnaissance, command and control, autonomous systems, and cyber and
information operations. It maps the technical, ethical, legal, and
geopolitical risk landscape, and proposes a lifecycle governance model
encompassing requirements, design, testing, deployment, and
decommissioning. The paper articulates core principles — lawfulness,
responsibility, reliability, and democratic accountability — and
translates them into institutional mechanisms and international
norm-building measures. Policy recommendations are directed at national
governments, defense ministries, and multilateral bodies. The central
argument is that responsible military AI governance is not merely a
technical challenge but a constitutional, ethical, and geopolitical one.
States that lead on governance will shape the norms that all actors
eventually must live with.

1. Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is rapidly transforming military and security
capabilities — from intelligence analysis and logistics to autonomous
systems and cyber operations. Governments face a dual imperative:
maintain strategic advantage while preventing misuse, escalation, and
systemic harm.

This white paper outlines:

- Current and emerging military AI use cases across all operational
  domains

- Key risks spanning technical, ethical, legal, and geopolitical
  dimensions

- Governance and oversight mechanisms appropriate to the nature and
  stakes of military AI

- Policy and standards recommendations for responsible, accountable
  deployment

Core Argument: Responsible military AI governance is not merely a
technical challenge — it is a constitutional, ethical, and geopolitical
imperative. States that lead on governance will shape the norms that all
actors eventually must live with.

2. Context and Drivers

The accelerating integration of AI into national security operations is
driven by a convergence of strategic, technological, and political
forces. Understanding these drivers is essential to designing governance
frameworks that are both effective and durable.

- Strategic competition: States view AI as a decisive enabler for
  deterrence, power projection, and decision superiority. The perception
  that rivals are investing heavily in military AI creates powerful
  incentives to accelerate domestic programs, often ahead of adequate
  oversight structures.

- Data and compute abundance: Intelligence, surveillance, and
  reconnaissance (ISR) data, satellite feeds, and cyber telemetry now
  exceed human analytic capacity by orders of magnitude. AI offers the
  only scalable means of deriving actionable insight from this volume
  and velocity of information.

- Political pressure: Leaders are incentivized to demonstrate they are
  not falling behind rivals in the AI competition, creating conditions
  in which deployment timelines routinely outpace the development of
  governance frameworks, safety validation, and legal review.

These drivers operate simultaneously and reinforce one another,
producing a systemic bias toward faster adoption and a corresponding
underinvestment in the institutional infrastructure needed to govern AI
responsibly at scale.

3. Core Military AI Use Cases

Military AI applications span the full spectrum of operations, from
strategic intelligence to tactical engagement. The following use cases
represent the most consequential and widely deployed categories as of
2026, and are the primary subject of governance concern.

3.1 Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance (ISR)

AI has become foundational to ISR operations, enabling the processing
and analysis of data streams that no human workforce could manage
manually. Key applications include:

- Pattern recognition: Object detection and classification in satellite
  imagery, drone video feeds, and synthetic aperture radar — identifying
  vehicles, fortifications, troop movements, and infrastructure changes
  at scale.

- Signal and cyber intelligence: Anomaly detection within communications
  traffic, network behavioral analysis, and automated attribution of
  cyber intrusion activity.

- Threat prediction: Predictive models trained on historical and
  real-time data to forecast force movements, escalation indicators, and
  adversary decision patterns.

3.2 Command, Control, and Decision Support

AI is increasingly embedded in the decision-support layer of military
command architectures, with significant implications for the speed and
nature of military decision-making:

- Decision-support systems: Automated course-of-action (COA) analysis,
  wargaming simulation, logistics optimization, and resource allocation
  modelling to support commanders under time pressure.

- Battlefield awareness: Real-time fusion of multi-domain sensor data
  across land, sea, air, cyber, and space domains, presented in unified
  operational pictures with AI-generated assessments and
  recommendations.

3.3 Autonomous and Semi-Autonomous Systems

The development and deployment of autonomous systems represents the most
ethically contested frontier of military AI, engaging fundamental
questions of legal compliance and human responsibility:

- Uncrewed aerial, surface, and underwater vehicles employed for
  reconnaissance, electronic warfare, logistics resupply, and — in some
  configurations — kinetic strike missions.

- Loitering munitions and collaborative drone swarms with varying and
  sometimes graduated degrees of autonomous target selection and
  engagement authority.

3.4 Cyber and Information Operations

- Offensive capabilities: Automated vulnerability discovery, exploit
  generation, and adaptive intrusion systems operating at machine speed.

- Defensive AI: Intrusion detection and response, automated patch
  deployment, and the creation of adaptive deception environments
  (honeypots, decoy networks) to mislead adversaries.

- Information operations: Synthetic media generation, micro-targeted
  influence campaigns, and counter-disinformation tools designed to
  detect and degrade adversarial narratives at scale.

4. Risk Landscape

The risks associated with military AI are multi-dimensional and mutually
reinforcing. Effective governance must address each category,
recognising that failures in one domain typically compound risks in
others.

4.1 Technical and Operational Risks

- Model brittleness and classification error: AI systems trained on
  historical or synthetic data may fail unpredictably under novel
  operational conditions — misclassifying civilians as combatants,
  friendly vehicles as adversary assets, or benign activity as
  threatening.

- Data quality and systemic bias: Skewed, incomplete, or adversarially
  manipulated training data can produce AI outputs that are
  systematically unreliable or discriminatory in ways that are difficult
  to detect until harm has occurred.

- Human–machine teaming failures: Automation bias — the tendency to
  over-trust AI outputs — is a well-documented failure mode. Conversely,
  under-trust or poor interface design can cause operators to ignore
  accurate AI alerts, undermining the value of the system entirely.

4.2 Ethical and Legal Risks

- Accountability gaps: Existing legal frameworks assign responsibility
  for unlawful harm to identifiable human actors. When AI systems
  contribute to lethal decisions across complex kill chains, the
  diffusion of responsibility creates gaps that current law does not
  adequately fill.

- Compliance with international humanitarian law (IHL): The IHL
  principles of distinction (between combatants and civilians),
  proportionality (in collateral harm), and precaution (in attack
  planning) require human judgement that autonomous systems may be
  incapable of reliably exercising in complex or ambiguous environments.

- Dignity and the limits of delegation: The delegation of life-and-death
  decisions to machine systems raises profound ethical questions about
  the moral weight of human life and whether such delegation is
  compatible with the foundational values of international law and
  democratic societies.

4.3 Strategic and Geopolitical Risks

- Arms race dynamics: Competitive pressure to deploy immature or
  inadequately tested systems to avoid perceived strategic disadvantage
  creates a race-to-the-bottom on safety and governance that is
  difficult for any single state to exit unilaterally.

- Escalation and miscalculation: The speed and opacity of AI-enabled
  military systems in crisis scenarios increases the risk of unintended
  escalation — particularly when adversaries cannot accurately interpret
  or attribute AI-driven actions.

- Proliferation and diffusion: Dual-use AI models and military-origin
  tools risk diffusion to non-state actors, authoritarian governments,
  and criminal organisations, extending the harm horizon well beyond the
  original deployment context.

Risk Compounding: Technical failures can trigger legal violations; legal
gaps can entrench bad practices; and the absence of international norms
allows dangerous technical practices to proliferate. These risk
categories are not independent — they amplify one another. Governance
frameworks must address all three in concert.

5. Governance and Oversight Framework

A governance-first approach to military AI requires more than policy
declarations — it demands institutional architecture, enforceable
standards, and a lifecycle approach that embeds oversight from the
earliest stages of development through to decommissioning. This section
proposes a three-layer framework: guiding principles, institutional
mechanisms, and lifecycle controls.

5.1 Principles for Military AI

The following principles should serve as the foundational commitments
from which all downstream governance requirements are derived:

- Lawful: All military AI systems must be designed, tested, and deployed
  in full compliance with applicable domestic law and international
  humanitarian law. Legal review must precede deployment, not follow it.

- Responsible: Clear and non-diffuse human accountability must be
  maintained across the entire kill chain or decision chain in which AI
  operates. Full traceability of AI-influenced decisions must be
  technically guaranteed.

- Reliable and Secure: Systems must be subject to rigorous testing,
  adversarial red-teaming, and ongoing cyber resilience assessment
  before and during operational deployment.

- Aligned with Democratic Values: Military AI programs must be subject
  to meaningful parliamentary and congressional oversight, and — where
  consistent with national security — public transparency about their
  existence, scope, and safeguards.

5.2 Institutional Mechanisms

- Civilian oversight: Parliamentary and congressional committees must
  develop genuine technical expertise in AI — not merely procedural
  oversight — to provide meaningful scrutiny of defense AI acquisition
  and deployment decisions.

- Independent review bodies: Dedicated AI ethics boards, standing
  red-team units with access to live systems, and independent safety
  review panels should be established outside the operational chain of
  command to preserve impartiality.

- Interagency coordination: Defense, foreign affairs, intelligence
  community, and national standards bodies must operate in concert, with
  shared frameworks rather than fragmented departmental policies that
  create governance gaps at the seams.

5.3 Lifecycle Controls

Governance cannot be retrofitted onto AI systems after deployment. The
following controls must be embedded at each phase of the system
lifecycle:

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Lifecycle Phase                     Required Controls
  ----------------------------------- -----------------------------------
  Requirements                        Explicit articulation of legal,
                                      ethical, and safety constraints as
                                      non-negotiable design requirements
                                      prior to any development contract
                                      award.

  Design & Development                Threat modelling, safety case
                                      documentation, model cards, and
                                      formal documentation of known
                                      limitations, failure modes, and
                                      adversarial vulnerabilities.

  Testing & Evaluation                Scenario-based operational testing,
                                      adversarial red-teaming,

  Decommissioning                     Defined and pre-agreed criteria for

                                      be unsafe, persistently unreliable,
                                      or rendered obsolete by technical
                                      or legal developments.

6. International Norms and Agreements

No single state can resolve the governance challenges of military AI
unilaterally. The systemic nature of the risks — arms race dynamics,
escalation, proliferation — requires coordinated international action.
This section surveys the existing legal landscape and identifies the
most promising avenues for norm development.

6.1 Existing Legal Framework

The foundational instruments of international law apply to AI-enabled
military systems regardless of whether they were drafted with AI in
mind:

- The UN Charter: Governs the use of force, including the prohibition of
  aggression and the right of self-defence — principles that apply
  equally to AI-enabled kinetic operations.

- International Humanitarian Law: The Geneva Conventions and their
  Additional Protocols impose binding obligations of distinction,
  proportionality, and precaution that must be met by any weapon system,
  whether operated by a human or an algorithm.

- International Human Rights Law: Applies in all circumstances, imposing
  requirements of legality, necessity, and proportionality on lethal
  force even outside armed conflict — including in AI-enabled law
  enforcement and border security contexts.

6.2 Emerging Norms

- Political declarations: A growing number of states have issued
  political declarations on responsible military AI use, including
  commitments to meaningful human control, legal review, and restraint
  in autonomous weapons development. These declarations are not legally
  binding but represent an evolving normative baseline.

- Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS) at the UN: Ongoing
  multilateral discussions under the Convention on Certain Conventional
  Weapons (CCW) Group of Governmental Experts have produced no binding
  agreement but have established shared vocabulary and identified key
  areas of concern, including the meaning and scope of "meaningful human
  control."

6.3 Confidence-Building Measures

In the absence of comprehensive treaty frameworks, confidence-building
measures can reduce the risk of miscalculation and lay the groundwork
for more durable agreements:

- Transparency reporting: States publishing transparency reports on
  their military AI testing protocols, safety standards, and doctrinal
  frameworks — without compromising operational security — contribute to
  a common understanding of acceptable practice.

- Crisis communication channels: Dedicated hotlines and incident
  notification protocols for AI-related military incidents — analogous
  to the Cold War-era nuclear risk reduction centres — would reduce the
  risk of misattribution and inadvertent escalation.

- Joint exercises and verification pilots: Bilateral or multilateral
  exercises involving AI-enabled systems, with agreed observation and
  verification protocols, can build trust and demonstrate good-faith
  commitments to responsible use among allied states.

7. Policy Recommendations

The following recommendations are directed at three principal audiences:
national governments and legislatures; defense ministries and armed
forces; and multilateral and international bodies. They are intended to
be actionable within existing institutional structures while building
toward more durable long-term governance architectures.

7.1 For National Governments

- Codify a national military AI policy with explicit red lines — for
  example, a legislatively mandated prohibition on fully autonomous
  targeting systems operating without meaningful human control over the
  decision to use lethal force.

- Mandate independent safety and ethics review, conducted by bodies
  outside the operational chain of command, for all high-risk AI
  capabilities prior to any operational deployment.

- Require rigorous test, evaluation, verification, and validation (TEVV)
  against legally defined performance and safety thresholds as a
  prerequisite for any acquisition approval or deployment authority.

- Invest substantially in AI literacy programs for senior commanders,
  civilian policymakers, and legislative oversight bodies, ensuring that
  those responsible for governance possess the technical understanding
  to exercise it meaningfully.

7.2 For Defense Ministries and Armed Forces

- Adopt comprehensive internal AI governance frameworks, including
  standardised model cards, AI risk registers, and mandatory incident
  reporting protocols for all operational AI systems.

- Standardise and formally define human-in-the-loop and
  human-on-the-loop requirements for specific mission types, in
  accordance with the nature of the system's outputs and the
  consequences of its decisions.

- Institutionalise red-teaming and adversarial testing as a standard,
  resourced, and mandatory component of all AI acquisition and upgrade
  processes — not an optional or ad hoc activity.

- Integrate fail-safe mechanisms, system-level kill switches, and clear
  rules of engagement for all AI-enabled systems into acquisition
  contracts as non-negotiable technical requirements.

7.3 For International Cooperation

- Support and actively engage with multilateral forums dedicated to the
  responsible development and use of military AI, including the CCW
  Group of Governmental Experts on LAWS and emerging bilateral dialogue
  frameworks.

- Pursue interoperable technical standards for logging, auditability,
  and safety verification across allied systems, enabling joint
  operations while maintaining coherent accountability frameworks.

- Develop shared crisis protocols — agreed in advance — for responding
  to AI-related military incidents, including misfires, autonomous
  system malfunctions, and misattribution events, to reduce the risk of
  escalation under conditions of uncertainty.

Priority Action: The single highest-leverage action available to most
governments is the establishment of a mandatory, independent AI safety
and ethics review process as a gating condition for any high-risk
military AI capability entering operational service. No deployment
without review should become a firm institutional norm, not an
aspiration.

8. Conclusion

Military AI is not optional for states — it is already embedded in
intelligence processing, logistics management, and cyber operations
worldwide. The question before governments, armed forces, legislatures,
and international bodies is not whether to engage with military AI, but
how to govern it in ways that are lawful, accountable, and consistent
with the values of democratic societies and the rules of the
international order.

A governance-first approach is not a counsel of technological timidity.
It does not require states to forfeit strategic advantage or abandon
capability development. What it does require is that the institutional
infrastructure of governance — legal review, independent oversight,
safety testing, accountability mechanisms, and international norms — be
developed in parallel with, and as a condition of, the capabilities
themselves.

The risks of the alternative are not hypothetical. AI systems that
misclassify targets, operate without meaningful human oversight, or
accelerate crises beyond the pace of human decision-making represent
strategic liabilities, not just ethical failures. States that deploy AI
recklessly invite the kind of incidents that erode domestic trust,
damage alliances, and invite international condemnation.

Responsible military AI is not merely a technical challenge; it is a
constitutional, ethical, and geopolitical one. States that lead on
governance will shape the norms that everyone else will eventually have
to live with. The window for establishing durable, legitimate, and
broadly accepted frameworks remains open — but it is narrowing with each
year of uncoordinated deployment and deferred decision-making.

The Hollow House Institute calls on governments, defense ministries,
legislatures, and international bodies to treat AI governance not as a
constraint on military capability but as the condition under which such
capability can be lawfully, safely, and sustainably exercised. The
moment for principled leadership is now.

Central Finding: States that invest in governance infrastructure
alongside capability development will achieve more durable strategic
advantage than those that race to deploy without oversight — because
trust, legality, and international legitimacy are themselves strategic
assets.
