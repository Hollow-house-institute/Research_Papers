Foreword

Foreword
This compendium presents six integrated visual models developed by Hollow House Institute to formalize the relationship between observed behavior, governance authority, and the emergence of durable institutional infrastructure. These models do not describe governance as it is proclaimed — they describe governance as it operates: through pattern, recurrence, signal, and consequence.

Each visual is presented with interpretive commentary, structural description, and cross-reference to the other models in the series. Together, they constitute a unified theory of behavioral governance — one grounded in the conviction that behavior, not policy, is the foundational substrate of any functioning institution.

This document is intended for use by governance architects, institutional designers, compliance practitioners, standards bodies, and policy researchers engaged in the study and implementation of adaptive, behavior-aware governance systems.

Section I

Foundational Doctrine
Core Doctrine Statements — Hollow House Institute

“Time turns behavior into infrastructure.”

“Behavior is the most honest data there is.”

On the First Doctrine
Governance infrastructure is not built by mandate alone. It is built by repetition. When a behavior recurs across time, it ceases to be an event and becomes a pattern. When a pattern persists, it becomes an expectation. When an expectation is enforced, it becomes a norm. When a norm is codified and defended, it becomes infrastructure. This process is not metaphorical — it is the literal mechanism by which institutions form. Policy documents describe this process after the fact. The behavior precedes the record.

On the Second Doctrine
Stated policy is a declaration of intent. Behavior is a record of reality. These two are frequently misaligned — and the gap between them is the most diagnostically rich space available to governance analysts. When an organization claims to value transparency but consistently withholds information, behavior reveals the operative truth. When a governance body claims authority but is routinely bypassed, behavior reveals the actual authority structure. Behavioral data does not lie because it does not know it is being observed. It simply accumulates.

On Their Conjunction
Together, these doctrines establish the epistemological foundation for this compendium. If behavior is the most honest data, and time converts behavior into infrastructure, then the study of governance infrastructure is ultimately the study of what people have consistently done — and what those consistent acts have constructed. The six models that follow operationalize this insight across different architectural layers of institutional governance.

Section II

Visual Models
The six models in this section form an integrated system. They may be read sequentially as a logical progression — from the foundational feedloop, through the authority and runtime layers, into schema, drift detection, and long-range formation. They may also be read thematically by layer: structural models (I, II, III), data models (IV), and temporal models (V, VI). Cross-references between models are noted within each interpretation.

Figure 1  |  Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop

[FIGURE 1: Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop]

Circular diagram showing the self-reinforcing cycle:

Observed Behavior → Pattern Recognition → Norm Encoding
→ Infrastructure Enforcement → Behavioral Constraint → Observed Behavior

(Loop is continuous; no initiating event and no terminal endpoint)

Figure 1. The Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop depicts governance as a closed, self-reinforcing cycle of behavioral sedimentation.

The Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop is the conceptual core of this compendium. It depicts governance not as a static structure but as a self-reinforcing cycle in which behavior generates patterns, patterns produce norms, norms become infrastructure, and infrastructure in turn shapes future behavior. The loop is closed: there is no external initiating event and no terminal endpoint. Governance, in this model, is a continuous process of behavioral sedimentation.

The cycle proceeds through six identifiable phases. First, Observed Behavior — the raw signal layer, comprising individual acts, decisions, transactions, and interactions within a governed environment. Second, Pattern Recognition — the analytical layer, in which recurrence is detected across actors, time periods, or contexts. Third, Norm Encoding — the codification layer, wherein detected patterns are formalized as expectations, policies, or standards. Fourth, Infrastructure Enforcement — the operational layer, in which encoded norms are applied through mechanisms of consequence: approval workflows, automated controls, audit trails, or social sanction. Fifth, Behavioral Constraint — the feedback layer, wherein enforcement shapes the range of available or socially permissible behavior. Sixth, the loop closes as constrained behavior generates new observed behavioral signals, and the cycle continues.

This model has critical implications for governance diagnosis. System failures — compliance gaps, authority erosion, norm drift — most commonly originate not in the policy layer but in the behavioral signal layer: the wrong behaviors are being observed, or the right behaviors are not being observed at sufficient resolution. The feedloop makes visible what purely policy-oriented frameworks obscure: that governance quality is ultimately determined upstream of enforcement, at the point of behavioral detection.

The feedloop also illuminates the danger of governance lag. When the distance between Observed Behavior and Norm Encoding is too great — when behavioral signals accumulate without pattern recognition, or patterns are recognized without codification — the infrastructure layer falls behind the behavioral reality it is meant to govern. The gap that opens between actual behavior and enforced norms is the primary incubator of institutional dysfunction.

Cross-reference: The feedloop's enforcement layer corresponds to the Authority Stack's Tier III (Operational Authority). Its pattern recognition phase corresponds to the behavioral signal processing described in Figure 4, Canonical Governance Event Schema.

Tiered pyramid diagram with five tiers, base to apex:

(I) Behavioral Substrate   ·   (II) Operational Authority
(III) Policy Authority   ·   (IV) Constitutional Authority
(V) Meta-Governance

Figure 2. The Governance Authority Stack places behavioral substrate at the foundation of all institutional authority.

The Governance Authority Stack formalizes the layered architecture of institutional authority. It distinguishes five tiers arranged hierarchically, with each tier deriving its legitimacy from those below it while simultaneously constraining those above. The Stack's primary conceptual contribution is its placement of behavioral substrate at the foundation — challenging the conventional assumption that authority flows downward from constitutional or policy documents.

Tier I — Behavioral Substrate is the foundation layer. It comprises the accumulated behavioral patterns of actors within the governed system: what people actually do, how they actually communicate, which decisions they actually make, and which rules they actually follow. This tier is not formally enacted — it emerges. It is not visible in org charts — it is legible only through longitudinal behavioral observation. Yet it is the most powerful tier in the stack, because every layer above it ultimately depends on behavioral compliance to remain operative.

Tier II — Operational Authority encompasses the day-to-day mechanisms through which governance is enacted: workflow approvals, role-based permissions, audit checkpoints, escalation procedures, and automated controls. This is the tier that most practitioners experience as governance. It is also the tier most vulnerable to behavioral override — when Tier I behaviors systematically circumvent Tier II mechanisms, the operational layer becomes theatrical.

Tier III — Policy Authority comprises the formal written instruments of governance: policies, procedures, standards, and guidelines. These documents encode the normative expectations of the institution. Their authority is real but contingent: policies that are not reflected in Tier II operations and Tier I behaviors have no effective force, regardless of their formal standing.

Tier IV — Constitutional Authority and Tier V — Meta-Governance represent the highest-order governance structures: organizational charters, governing board mandates, regulatory frameworks, and the mechanisms by which governance itself is designed, evaluated, and reformed. These tiers set the parameters within which all lower tiers operate. Their stability depends on the integrity of the tiers below them — a constitutional authority is only as durable as the behavioral substrate that sustains it.

Cross-reference: Tier I of the Authority Stack is the direct output of the Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop (Figure 1). Tier II operational mechanisms are the primary domain of the Runtime Governance Architecture (Figure 3).
Figure 3  |  Runtime Governance Architecture

[FIGURE 3: Runtime Governance Architecture]

Layered systems diagram with feedback arrows between each layer:

Behavioral Signal Layer ↓ → Event Processing Layer ↓ → Rule Evaluation Engine
↓ → Decision and Enforcement Layer ↓ → Audit and Memory Layer

Bidirectional feedback arrows connect each layer to adjacent layers.

Figure 3. The Runtime Governance Architecture depicts the operational execution of governance from signal intake through enforcement and audit persistence.

The Runtime Governance Architecture translates the conceptual models of Figures 1 and 2 into an operational systems diagram. Where the Feedloop describes the logic of governance and the Authority Stack describes its structure, the Runtime Architecture describes its execution: how governance actually processes behavioral signals, evaluates them against encoded rules, and produces consequential decisions in real time.

The architecture is organized into five functional layers. The Behavioral Signal Layer is the intake surface — the point at which raw behavioral events enter the governance processing system. These events may be digital (transactions, access logs, communication records) or analog (observed decisions, meeting patterns, resource allocation choices). The signal layer's primary functions are detection and capture: ensuring that the right signals are visible to the system at sufficient resolution and timeliness.

The Event Processing Layer receives raw signals and transforms them into structured governance events. This layer performs normalization, deduplication, enrichment, and preliminary classification. Its output is a stream of typed, attributed, and timestamped events that the rule evaluation layer can process. The quality of governance decisions downstream is directly determined by the fidelity of event processing upstream.

The Rule Evaluation Engine is the analytical core of the architecture. It receives processed events and evaluates them against the institution's encoded governance rules: thresholds, policies, behavioral expectations, and contextual conditions. The engine produces one of three outputs for each evaluated event: compliant (no action required), flagged (requires review), or non-compliant (requires enforcement action). Sophisticated implementations of this engine maintain probabilistic models of expected behavior, enabling detection of anomalous patterns that no single rule would capture.

The Decision and Enforcement Layer receives rule evaluation outputs and executes consequential actions: approval or rejection of requests, escalation to human reviewers, automated remediation, or formal sanction. The Audit and Memory Layer persists all events, evaluations, decisions, and enforcement actions to a durable record — the institutional memory that enables retrospective analysis, drift detection, and the long-range behavioral pattern analysis depicted in Figures 5 and 6.

Cross-reference: The Event Processing Layer outputs the event types formally defined in Figure 4, Canonical Governance Event Schema. The Audit and Memory Layer is the primary data source for the Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5) and the Infrastructure Formation model (Figure 6).

Figure 4. The Canonical Governance Event Schema provides the formal data specification for all governance events within the Runtime Architecture.

The Canonical Governance Event Schema provides the formal data specification for governance events as they are processed within the Runtime Architecture (Figure 3). It answers a foundational question that most governance systems leave implicit: what, precisely, is a governance event, and what attributes must it carry to support reliable processing, audit, and pattern analysis?

The schema defines a governance event as a structured, typed record of a discrete behavioral occurrence that is material to governance evaluation. The core object contains the following canonical fields: event_id (globally unique identifier), timestamp (high-precision occurrence time), actor_id (the entity whose behavior generated the event), event_type (a controlled vocabulary of six types: DECISION, ACTION, SIGNAL, VIOLATION, REMEDIATION, and AUDIT), context_scope (the organizational or systemic context within which the event occurred), authority_tier (the tier of the Authority Stack to which the event is attributable), behavioral_vector (a structured encoding of the behavioral pattern the event instantiates or reinforces), confidence_score (a probabilistic estimate of classification accuracy), and linked_events (a list of event IDs representing temporal or causal relationships to other governance events).

The event_type enumeration deserves particular attention. DECISION events record consequential choices made by actors with governance authority. ACTION events record behavioral acts that may or may not be consequential but are material to pattern analysis. SIGNAL events record weak or ambiguous behavioral indicators that do not individually trigger enforcement but contribute to pattern recognition. VIOLATION events record confirmed departures from encoded norms. REMEDIATION events record corrective actions taken in response to violations. AUDIT events record governance system observations and self-referential checks.

The behavioral_vector field is the schema's most analytically powerful element. Rather than recording behavior as a simple description, the behavioral vector encodes behavior as a directional value in a multi-dimensional behavioral space — enabling the governance system to measure not just what happened, but how this event relates to the trajectory of behavioral patterns over time. This vector representation is what enables the drift detection capabilities depicted in Figure 5.

The schema is designed to be technology-agnostic and implementation-flexible. It represents the minimum canonical structure required for interoperable governance data — the common language through which behavioral signals can be shared, compared, and analyzed across systems, institutions, and time horizons.

Cross-reference: Events of type SIGNAL and DECISION feed directly into the behavioral pattern analysis depicted in the Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5). The behavioral_vector field is the primary input to the Infrastructure Formation model (Figure 6).

Figure 5. The Behavioral Drift Timeline renders the progressive divergence of actual behavior from established governance baselines as a measurable, observable object.

The Behavioral Drift Timeline introduces the temporal dimension of governance analysis. Where Figures 1 through 4 describe the structure and mechanics of governance systems, Figure 5 asks: what happens when behavior moves? How do governance systems detect, respond to, and ultimately incorporate gradual departures from established behavioral baselines?

Behavioral drift is the progressive divergence of observed behavior from an established norm or baseline over time. It differs from violation in a critical respect: a violation is a discrete departure, detectable in a single event. Drift is a trajectory — legible only across a sequence of events. A single observation of slightly elevated risk behavior may be noise; the same observation repeated with increasing frequency over six months is drift. The Behavioral Drift Timeline makes this trajectory visible as a measurement object.

The timeline identifies five characteristic phases of a drift event. Initial Drift is the earliest detectable departure from baseline — often below enforcement thresholds and attributable to noise by automated systems. This phase is the highest-value intervention point and the most frequently missed. Drift Amplification occurs when initial drift is not corrected and the departing trajectory becomes self-reinforcing, often because actors observe that the departure carries no consequence. Threshold Breach marks the point at which cumulative drift crosses the detection or enforcement threshold, triggering formal governance response. Enforcement Response is the period of consequential action. Re-baseline describes the recalibration of the behavioral baseline following resolution — either restoring the original norm or formally encoding the new behavioral equilibrium as the new standard.

The Re-baseline phase is particularly significant. When governance systems respond to drift by updating the baseline to match the drifted behavior rather than enforcing return to the original norm, they are performing an implicit policy revision through behavioral accommodation. This process — norm erosion through unrecognized re-baselining — is one of the most common mechanisms by which institutional standards degrade. The Behavioral Drift Timeline makes this process observable and therefore governable.

Cross-reference: The drift data visualized in this timeline is generated from behavioral_vector values accumulated in the Canonical Governance Event Schema (Figure 4). Sustained drift that results in Re-baselining feeds directly into the long-range formation patterns depicted in Figure 6.

Figure 6. The Infrastructure Formation model employs a stratigraphic representation to depict how behavioral recurrence hardens, over time, into constitutional substrate.

Infrastructure Formation Over Time is the long-range view — the model that shows what happens when the feedloop (Figure 1) runs for years or decades. The geological metaphor is intentional and precise: just as physical strata form through the accumulation of sediment over time, governance infrastructure forms through the accumulation of behavioral events that harden, layer by layer, into institutional substrate.

The diagram uses a stratigraphic representation. At the surface — most recent, most volatile — lies Ephemeral Behavior: individual acts that have not yet recurred with sufficient frequency to constitute a pattern. Below it, Recurrent Pattern: behavioral sequences that have appeared consistently enough to be recognizable but not yet formally encoded. Deeper still, Emergent Norm: patterns that actors within the system have begun to treat as expectations, even in the absence of formal codification. Then Codified Policy: norms that have been formally written, enacted, and attached to enforcement mechanisms. Below policy, Structural Infrastructure: the operational systems, processes, and organizational arrangements that have been built to implement and sustain policy. And at the deepest layer, Constitutional Substrate: the foundational arrangements — often no longer consciously examined — that define what governance in this institution is permitted to be.

This model has profound implications for governance reform. Reformers frequently target the Codified Policy layer, reasoning that changing the written rule will change behavior. The stratigraphic model predicts that this will fail — not because the policy change is wrong, but because the behavioral substrate beneath it has not changed. Policy revisions that are not accompanied by behavioral interventions at the Emergent Norm and Recurrent Pattern layers will be absorbed, neutralized, or rejected by the deeper strata they have not disturbed.

Effective governance reform must be stratigraphic in its approach: identifying which layer the problem actually lives in, and intervening at that layer and all layers above it simultaneously. A constitutional revision that is not expressed in structural infrastructure, codified policy, emerging norms, and ultimately in recurrent behavioral patterns has accomplished nothing except the revision of text.

The Infrastructure Formation model also explains why governance institutions are simultaneously more durable and more brittle than they appear. They are durable because the lower strata are genuinely resistant to surface-level disruption. They are brittle because when the lower strata do shift — when the behavioral substrate beneath an institution changes dramatically — the entire superstructure above it is destabilized, often suddenly and without warning.

Cross-reference: The strata in this model correspond directly to the tiers of the Governance Authority Stack (Figure 2). The accumulation process depicted here is the long-run output of the Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop (Figure 1). The rate of stratum formation can be measured through longitudinal analysis of the Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5).
Section III

The Models as a System
The six models presented in this compendium are not a collection of related diagrams — they are a unified analytical system in which each model occupies a distinct and non-redundant position. The Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop (Figure 1) provides the foundational logic: the mechanism by which behavior becomes infrastructure. The Governance Authority Stack (Figure 2) provides the structural architecture: the layered arrangement of authority types that the feedloop produces and sustains. The Runtime Governance Architecture (Figure 3) provides the operational layer: the processing system through which behavioral signals are converted into governance decisions in real time. The Canonical Governance Event Schema (Figure 4) provides the data model: the formal specification of the information objects that flow through the runtime architecture. The Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5) provides the temporal diagnostic instrument: the means by which movement in the behavioral substrate is rendered visible and measurable. And the Infrastructure Formation model (Figure 6) provides the long-range perspective: the stratigraphic view of what the feedloop constructs over years and decades. Data flows continuously between these models. Behavioral events captured by the signal layer (Figure 3) are structured according to the schema (Figure 4), their vectors aggregated into the drift timeline (Figure 5), and their cumulative effects expressed in the formation strata (Figure 6). The authority stack (Figure 2) provides the structural reference frame within which all of this occurs, and the feedloop (Figure 1) describes the dynamic that drives all of it forward. Remove any one model and the system loses a dimension of governance legibility it cannot recover through the others.

The central claim of this system — its primary theoretical contribution — is that behavioral observation is the primary epistemic act of governance. All other governance functions are derivative of the quality and fidelity of behavioral observation. Authority cannot be properly situated without behavioral data telling us where operative decisions are actually being made. Enforcement cannot be properly calibrated without behavioral data telling us which norms are actually being followed. Policy cannot be properly evaluated without behavioral data telling us whether its provisions are reflected in practice. Audit cannot be meaningful without behavioral data sufficient to distinguish genuine compliance from theatrical compliance. This claim is not a normative preference — it is a structural fact about how governance systems process reality. A governance institution that does not observe behavior with rigor is not merely incomplete: it is systematically blind to the substrate on which everything it does depends. The six models operationalize the corrective: they provide the conceptual tools, data structures, and analytical instruments necessary to make behavioral observation a first-class governance function rather than an afterthought.

The diagnostic utility of the six-model system is most clearly expressed through the sequence in which the models are applied when a governance failure is detected or suspected. The practitioner begins with the Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5), which is the instrument most likely to surface the earliest evidence of a governance problem — a departure from baseline that precedes and predicts the more visible failures that will follow. Once drift is detected, the practitioner traces back through the Canonical Governance Event Schema (Figure 4) to examine the raw event record: which actor types are generating the anomalous signals, at which authority tiers, and with what behavioral vectors. This analysis informs evaluation of the Runtime Governance Architecture (Figure 3): is the signal layer capturing the relevant behaviors at sufficient resolution? Is the event processing layer correctly classifying them? Is the rule evaluation engine applying the right thresholds? From there, the practitioner maps the failure to the Governance Authority Stack (Figure 2): which tier is the operative locus of the problem — a behavioral substrate issue, an operational mechanism failure, or a policy authority breakdown? Finally, the practitioner determines whether the observed failure is primarily a feedloop problem (Figure 1) — a break in the cycle connecting behavior to norm to enforcement — or a formation problem (Figure 6) — a deeper stratum that has shifted in ways that are destabilizing the layers above it. This diagnostic sequence converts the six models from reference diagrams into a structured investigation protocol for governance failure analysis.

The normative implications of committing to this system are significant, and governance institutions should approach them with clarity about what is required. First, institutions must be willing to accept that behavioral evidence may contradict stated policy — and that when it does, the behavioral evidence is the more authoritative description of operative reality. This is not comfortable. It requires a willingness to acknowledge gaps between what an institution says it does and what it actually does, and to treat those gaps as the primary diagnostic signal rather than as embarrassments to be managed. Second, institutions must accept that norm erosion is frequently invisible until examined stratigraphically — that the standard being eroded often looks, at the policy layer, entirely intact, precisely because the erosion is occurring in the behavioral substrate beneath it. Periodic policy review will not detect this. Only longitudinal behavioral observation will. Third, genuine governance integrity under this framework requires continuous behavioral observation, not periodic policy review. The institutions capable of living by these commitments are those capable of governing themselves honestly — which is to say, those capable of seeing what they are actually doing, not merely what they intend to do.

Section V

Glossary of Terms
Behavioral Substrate

The foundational layer of governance infrastructure, comprising the accumulated behavioral patterns of actors within a governed system. Distinguished from policy or procedure in that it is not formally enacted but emerges through recurrence over time. The behavioral substrate is the most diagnostic layer of governance analysis because it reflects operative reality rather than declared intent. In the Governance Authority Stack (Figure 2), it constitutes Tier I — the layer upon which all other authority tiers depend for their operative legitimacy. Its content is legible not through documentation review but through longitudinal behavioral observation.

Governance Event

A discrete, structured record of a behavioral occurrence material to governance evaluation. As defined in the Canonical Governance Event Schema (Figure 4), a governance event carries typed classification, actor attribution, temporal precision, contextual scope, and a behavioral vector enabling pattern analysis. Events are the atomic unit of governance data within the Runtime Architecture (Figure 3) and the primary input to drift detection (Figure 5) and infrastructure formation analysis (Figure 6). The six canonical event types — DECISION, ACTION, SIGNAL, VIOLATION, REMEDIATION, and AUDIT — collectively constitute the vocabulary of observable governance behavior.

Behavioral Drift

The progressive divergence of observed behavior from an established governance baseline over time. Distinguished from violation by its gradual, cumulative nature: drift is legible only across a sequence of events and requires longitudinal observation for detection. A single departure from baseline may constitute noise or an isolated violation; drift is characterized by directionality, persistence, and self-amplification. The Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5) provides the analytical instrument for rendering drift visible, measuring its trajectory, and identifying its characteristic phases. Undetected drift is the primary mechanism through which governance standards erode without triggering formal enforcement responses.

Infrastructure Formation

The process by which recurrent behavioral patterns harden, through time, into institutional norms, codified policies, operational structures, and ultimately constitutional substrate. Depicted stratigraphically in Figure 6, infrastructure formation is the long-run output of the Behavior-Infrastructure Feedloop (Figure 1). The process is cumulative and largely self-organizing: it does not require deliberate architectural intention to produce genuine institutional structure. This property explains both the durability of established governance institutions and the difficulty of reforming them — the infrastructure formed by behavioral accumulation is resistant to intervention at layers that do not reach its foundational strata.

Authority Tier

A discrete layer within the Governance Authority Stack (Figure 2), characterized by a specific type of governance authority, mechanism of enforcement, and relationship to behavioral substrate. Authority tiers are hierarchically ordered but are legitimated from below — each tier's operative authority depends on the behavioral compliance of the tiers beneath it. The five canonical tiers are: Behavioral Substrate (Tier I), Operational Authority (Tier II), Policy Authority (Tier III), Constitutional Authority (Tier IV), and Meta-Governance (Tier V). The distinction between formal and operative authority is most sharply revealed when upper-tier declarations are not sustained by lower-tier behavioral compliance.

Runtime Governance

The operational execution of governance functions in real time, as distinct from the design or documentation of governance policy. Runtime governance encompasses behavioral signal intake, event processing, rule evaluation, enforcement decision, and audit persistence, as depicted in the Runtime Governance Architecture (Figure 3). The concept emphasizes that governance is not a document or a structure but a continuous operational process — one that must be running, receiving inputs, and producing outputs at all times in order to be functionally present. An institution whose governance exists only in policy documents and not in runtime execution has policy but does not have governance.

Re-baselining

The recalibration of a behavioral baseline following a drift event. Re-baselining may represent either the restoration of the original norm (positive re-baselining, following effective enforcement response) or the accommodation of the drifted behavior as the new norm (negative re-baselining, a primary mechanism of institutional standard erosion). Negative re-baselining is particularly dangerous because it is frequently invisible: the governance system updates its expectations to match degraded behavioral reality without a formal policy decision authorizing the change. The Behavioral Drift Timeline (Figure 5) makes re-baselining events observable, enabling governance institutions to distinguish deliberate baseline updates from inadvertent norm erosion.

Behavioral Vector

A structured, multi-dimensional encoding of a behavioral event that represents not only what the behavior was but its direction and magnitude relative to established baselines. The behavioral vector enables the governance system to detect and measure drift trajectories rather than simply recording discrete incidents, as defined in the Canonical Governance Event Schema (Figure 4). By representing each behavioral event as a point in a multi-dimensional space, the vector formalism allows the governance system to compute the trajectory of behavioral change over time — transforming raw event data into the drift measurements visualized in Figure 5 and the formation analyses depicted in Figure 6.
